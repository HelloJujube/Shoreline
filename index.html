Saline Conti Vecchi

Dialectic
of the Shore

Heritage as Infrastructure

On the south-western coast of Sardinia, the ancient salt pans of Conti Vecchi have operated continuously for over a century — a landscape built by labour, shaped by tide, and suspended between obsolescence and reinvention. This research traces the site as a living dialectic: a heritage that is also infrastructure, a ruin that is also a machine.

01

The Geometry of Salt

The pans read from above as pure geometry — rectangular basins subdivided by earthen levees, their colours shifting from pale chalk to deep magenta depending on salinity and the micro-organisms that inhabit each stage of evaporation. The grid is not decorative; it is thermodynamic logic made visible from the air.

02

A Century of Labour

Production at Conti Vecchi peaked in the mid-twentieth century, when thousands of workers — many of them women — harvested salt by hand before mechanisation arrived. Their seasonal rhythms, their vernacular shelters, and their collective knowledge constitute a form of intangible heritage that the built fabric can only partially index.

The Body as Instrument

Archival photographs reveal the body calibrated to the landscape: broad-brimmed hats against glare, bare feet on crystalline crust, the rake held at a precise angle to avoid breaking the pan floor. Knowledge lived in posture as much as in process.

03

Infrastructure as Monument

The hydraulic system that feeds the pans — a network of pumps, sluices, and graduated channels — is itself a monument to industrial modernity. Designed in the 1920s by the Montecatini chemical company, it remains largely intact, a palimpsest of engineering ambition now listed for its cultural significance.

04

Ecology by Accident

What industry made, nature colonised. Flamingos arrived at Conti Vecchi in the 1990s and now nest in numbers that make the site one of the most significant wetland reserves on the island. The managed water levels that serve salt production also happen to replicate the shallow-brine habitat flamingos require.

Heritage and the Non-Human

Conservation frameworks typically centre the human: the artefact, the memory, the community. Here the non-human presses back — demanding that any future for the site account for migration patterns, nesting calendars, and salinity tolerances alongside the claims of cultural significance.

05

Adaptive Re-use and its Contradictions

The former workers' village has been partially converted into a boutique hotel and museum. The conversion is careful — original finishes preserved, industrial artefacts recontextualised as display objects — yet it produces a new friction: the heritage of labour repackaged for the leisure economy that displaced it.

The Visitor and the Worker

Whose shore is this now? The dialectic the title names is not resolved by renovation. It sharpens. Heritage becomes infrastructure for tourism rather than production, and the question of who benefits — and who is narrated into the past — remains open.

06

Futures in Suspension

Climate projections threaten both. Rising sea levels may flood the lowest pans within decades; summer heat is already altering evaporation rates. The infrastructure built to manage water must now adapt to water it was never designed to handle. Heritage and urgency converge on the same levee.

Shore as Proposition

To study Conti Vecchi is to study the shore as a condition rather than a boundary — a zone of negotiation between states, between epochs, between the interests of capital, culture, ecology, and community. The dialectic does not resolve. It continues.
